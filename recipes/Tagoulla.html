Tagoulla: A Signature Amazigh Dish from Morocco

Description

Tagoulla, also known fondly as 'Aych', is more than just food for the Amazigh people; it is part of their cultural identity, heritage, and traditions. Native to the Souss-Massa region and beloved throughout the High Atlas, Anti-Atlas, and the Sous valley, Tagoulla's unpretentious preparation and versatility of ingredients—being made with corn, barley, or wheat flour—make it a robust symbol of the diverse Moroccan cuisine.

The utility and significance of Tagoulla cover immense ground—right from being a go-to meal for nomadic tribes to being served at special occasions. A noteworthy moment in the Amazigh calendar where this dish shines is the New Year celebrations, where the "Tagoulla of January" becomes a staple, bringing people together in joyous feasts.

Ingredients

  • 4 cups* of corn, barley, or wheat flour (depending on preference and region)
  • 1 liter* of water
  • 2* teaspoons of salt
  • A choice of honey, amlou (a blend of almonds, argan oil, and honey), or lidam (a form of fat), in addition to argan oil or butter

*The quantities mentioned are just a starting point, and can be adjusted according to the number of servings required, personal preferences, and regional customs.

Steps

  1. In a large clay pot, bring the water and salt to a boil over a fire.
  2. Gradually add the flour of your choice (corn, barley, or wheat), stirring constantly with a wooden spoon to avoid lumps.
  3. Keep stirring and cooking until the mixture transforms into a thick, smooth consistency. This usually takes around 20-35 minutes.
  4. Let the mixture cool slightly on a large plate or tray, then either using your hands or a knife, shape it into balls or loaves.
  5. Serve warm with a choice of argan oil, butter, honey, amlou, or lidam for dipping.

With every bite of Tagoulla, you get to experience a piece of Moroccan cultural heritage. This dish embodies more than just the Amazigh culinary tradition; it serves as a tangible link to Morocco’s rich cultural history, from its nomadic tribes to its festive celebrations.
